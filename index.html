Project Overview

Calculating Empires is a visual genealogy that traces relationships between technology, computation, colonialism, governance, labor, communication, and power from 1500 to the present.

Through an interactive website and a large-scale printed diagram, the project proposes a historical framework for understanding contemporary artificial intelligence and computational infrastructures.

Ontological Analysis

What exists in the project?

Calculating Empires maps the co-evolution of technology and systems of power over five centuries. Technology appears as an interconnected ecosystem of infrastructures, institutions, political systems, natural resources, human labor, and computational technologies.

Four fundamental concepts

  • Communication
  • Computation
  • Classification
  • Control

These expand into more than twenty thematic layers, including algorithms, data organization, colonialism, bureaucracy, policing, borders, architecture, military systems, surveillance infrastructures, and planetary resources.

Modes of existence

  • research archive
  • visual map
  • interactive website
  • large-scale physical installation

Its ontology is relational: meaning emerges through connections across time, rather than through individual technological artifacts.

Historical Context

Historical & Contextual Analysis

Developed in 2023, Calculating Empires responds to the rise of artificial intelligence, platform capitalism, and opaque computational infrastructures.

Instead of treating AI as a recent breakthrough, the project situates it in a trajectory beginning around 1500, when European colonial expansion, global trade networks, and the printing press transformed systems of communication and political power.

How did contemporary computational systems emerge, and what historical structures of power continue to shape them?

The project argues that today’s computational systems inherit historical structures of extraction, classification, surveillance, and colonial governance. Technological progress is not presented as a linear narrative: communications, institutions, economies, and imperial expansion continuously reinforce one another.

Visual & Aesthetic System

Visual Language

The visual language combines historical cartography, scientific diagrams, archival illustration, and information design. Thousands of hand-drawn elements, texts, and symbols form a timeline that demands exploration rather than quick consumption.

Representation Strategy

The composition uses two primary axes:

  • Vertical: time, 1500–today
  • Horizontal: communication, computation, classification, control

Users follow technologies, institutions, periods, or concepts to construct their own path.

Design References

The project draws from historical atlases, critical cartography, and large-scale diagrammatic systems, treating visual representation as both analytical and political.

Methodology

From historical inquiry to interactive genealogy

  1. Researchframes a long historical duration and identifies recurring patterns.
  2. Organizationclassifies material through themes, topics, time, and systems.
  3. Visualizationencodes relations through diagrams, drawings, and a thematic diptych.
  4. Interactionsupports navigation, zoom, slow reading, annotation, and plural interpretation.

Documented outcomes are distinguished from inferred production steps and unresolved archival or bibliographic procedures.
