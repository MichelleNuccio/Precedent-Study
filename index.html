CALCULATING EMPIRES

A Genealogy of Power and Technology, 1500-2025

Kate Crawford and Vladan Joler (2023)

ABOUT:

Interactive research visualization, large-scale printed map, exhibition, digital platform

INTENDED AUDIENCE:

Researchers, designers, artists, architects, students, policymakers, and the general public interested in technology, AI, colonialism, and systems of power.

PROJECT OVERVIEW:

Calculating Empires is a visual genealogy that traces the relationships between technology, computation, colonialism, governance, labor, communication, and power from 1500 to the present. Through an interactive website and a large-scale printed diagram, the project proposes a historical framework for understanding contemporary artificial intelligence and computational infrastructures.

ONTOLOGICAL ANALYSIS:

WHAT EXIST IN THE PROJECT?

Calculating Empires is a large-scale research visualization that maps the co-evolution of technology and systems of power over five centuries. Rather than presenting technology as an isolated phenomenon, the project constructs an interconnected ecosystem composed of infrastructures, institutions, political systems, natural resources, human labor, and computational technologies.

The visualization is organized around four fundamental concepts:

  • Communication
  • Classification
  • Control

These concepts are expanded into more than twenty thematic layers, including communication devices, algorithms, data organization, colonialism, bureaucracy, policing, borders, architecture, military systems, surveillance infrastructures, and planetary resources. Each layer contributes to a genealogy showing how technological systems evolve together with mechanisms of political and social control.

The project exists simultaneously as:

  • a research archive,
  • a visual map,
  • an interactive website,
  • and a large-scale physical installation.

Its ontology is therefore not object-based but relational: meaning emerges through connections across time rather than through individual technological artifacts.

HISTORICAL & CONTEXTUAL ANALYSIS:

Historical Context

Developed by Kate Crawford and Vladan Joler in 2023, Calculating Empires responds to the contemporary rise of artificial intelligence, platform capitalism, and increasingly opaque computational infrastructures. Instead of treating AI as a recent technological breakthrough, the project situates it within a continuous historical trajectory beginning around 1500, when European colonial expansion, global trade networks, and the printing press transformed systems of communication and political power.

The project argues that today's computational systems inherit historical structures of extraction, classification, surveillance, and colonial governance rather than representing entirely new forms of innovation.

Research Question

How did contemporary computational systems emerge, and what historical structures of power continue to shape them?

Instead of offering technological progress as a linear narrative, the project proposes a genealogy in which communication technologies, political institutions, economic systems, and imperial expansion continuously reinforce one another.

VISUAL & AESTHETIC REPRESENTATION:

Visual Language

The visual language combines historical cartography, scientific diagrams, archival illustration, and information design. Thousands of hand-drawn elements, texts, and symbols are arranged into an extensive timeline that requires exploration rather than quick consumption. The map functions less as an infographic and more as a visual research environment.

Representation Strategy

The composition is structured along two primary axes:

  • Vertical axis: time (1500-today)
  • Horizontal axis: thematic systems (communication, computation, classification, control)

Instead of guiding viewers through a single narrative, the visualization supports multiple reading paths. Users can follow technologies, institutions, historical periods, or conceptual themes, constructing their own interpretation through the network of relationships.

Design References

The representational strategy draws from traditions of visual knowledge production such as historical atlases, critical cartography, and large-scale diagrammatic systems. Crawford and Joler explicitly position the project within a lineage that includes Aby Warburg's Mnemosyne Atlas and Charles and Ray Eames' Mathematica, using visual representation as both an analytical and political tool.
