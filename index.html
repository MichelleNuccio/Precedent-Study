CALCULATING
EMPIRES

A Genealogy of Power and Technology from 1500 to the Present

Authors
Kate Crawford · Vladan Joler
Year
2023
Format
Interactive website, large-scale printed map, exhibition, research project.
Research Fields
Critical AI Studies, Information Visualization, Computational Design, Media Archaeology, Digital Humanities.

Abstract

Calculating Empires is a collaborative research project by Kate Crawford and Vladan Joler that investigates the historical genealogy of artificial intelligence and computational systems from 1500 to the present. Rather than presenting AI as an isolated technological breakthrough, the project situates it within a long history of infrastructures, extraction, labor, colonial expansion, communication networks, scientific knowledge, and political power. Through a large-scale printed map and an interactive digital interface, it assembles hundreds of historical actors, technologies, institutions, and events into a navigable visual archive. The project argues that contemporary AI is the result of centuries of interconnected technological, economic, and social developments rather than a recent innovation.

01_GENEALOGY.TXTRESEARCH RECORD

Overview

Introduction

Calculating Empires is the result of the convergence of two distinct but complementary research trajectories. Kate Crawford's work examines the political, environmental, and social dimensions of artificial intelligence, while Vladan Joler investigates the infrastructures of digital technologies through large-scale visual mapping and forensic research. Their collaboration combines critical theory, historical inquiry, information design, and investigative visualization into a single research framework. Rather than presenting AI as an isolated technological achievement, Calculating Empires reconstructs the long historical processes that made contemporary computational systems possible.

Australian researcher, writer, and scholar working across media studies, science and technology studies (STS), artificial intelligence, and critical data studies. Crawford has held research positions at institutions including Microsoft Research, MIT, USC Annenberg, and the AI Now Institute, where she co-founded one of the leading research centers investigating the social implications of artificial intelligence.

Genealogical Synthesis

The genealogy of Calculating Empires cannot be reduced to the biographies of its authors. Instead, it emerges from the intersection of two complementary practices: Crawford's critical investigation of artificial intelligence as a political and material system, and Joler's expertise in visualizing complex technological infrastructures. Their collaboration produces a hybrid methodology in which historical research, critical theory, investigative mapping, and information design become inseparable. The resulting project is therefore both a historical archive and a visual argument about the long-term evolution of technology and power.

06_Critical Reading

06_CRITICAL_READING.TXTINTERPRETIVE RECORD

Author's Argument

What is Calculating Empires arguing?

Calculating Empires challenges the widespread perception of artificial intelligence as a recent technological breakthrough. Instead, Crawford and Joler propose that AI should be understood as the outcome of a long historical process extending over five centuries. Through the reconstruction of interconnected infrastructures, institutions, technologies, labor systems, and political structures, the project argues that computation has always been embedded within broader systems of power.

Rather than producing a chronological history of technology, the project develops a genealogy that reveals continuity instead of rupture. Progress is presented not as a sequence of isolated innovations but as the cumulative result of extraction, classification, communication, scientific knowledge, colonial expansion, and economic organization. In doing so, the map questions narratives of technological neutrality and invites the viewer to understand artificial intelligence as a socio-technical and historical phenomenon.

Personal Interpretation

The Grid as Apparatus

My reading of Calculating Empires moves beyond its historical content and focuses on the structure through which knowledge is produced. The project does not simply represent history; it actively organizes it. The grid, the taxonomy, the hierarchy of categories, and the relationships between nodes constitute a mechanism that determines how information becomes visible and how historical connections are established.

From this perspective, the map can be understood as an apparatus (dispositivo), in the sense developed by Michel Foucault and later expanded by Giorgio Agamben. Rather than functioning as a neutral container of information, the visualization performs operations of selection, classification, and organization that shape the conditions under which knowledge is produced. The interface therefore becomes an active epistemological structure rather than a passive representational surface.

The project's strength lies precisely in making these operations visible. Instead of concealing the mechanisms through which history is constructed, the visualization exposes classification itself as a historical and political act.

Archive, Visibility and Control

The visual system resembles an archive more than a conventional historical timeline. Historical actors, technologies, institutions, and infrastructures are collected, classified, and connected within a common framework. This archival logic transforms scattered historical fragments into a coherent system that can be explored from multiple perspectives.

At the same time, every archive necessarily establishes conditions of visibility and exclusion. What becomes part of the genealogy is determined through acts of selection and categorization. The project therefore illustrates how archives are never neutral repositories of information but technologies that organize memory and produce particular forms of historical knowledge.

Rather than interpreting the archive as a static collection, I understand it as an active mechanism that continuously produces relationships between heterogeneous elements.

From Representation to Interface

One of the most significant aspects of Calculating Empires is the shift from representation to interaction. The digital interface does not simply reproduce the printed map but transforms it into an exploratory environment. Navigation, zooming, and free movement encourage users to construct their own genealogical pathways instead of following a predetermined narrative.

This principle directly informed the design of my precedent study. Rather than organizing the analysis as a sequence of webpages, I developed the website as a research workspace in which multiple records can remain open simultaneously. The interface reproduces the research process itself: users compare documents, establish connections between different analytical layers, and progressively construct their own understanding of the project.

In this sense, the website does not imitate the visual appearance of Calculating Empires; it translates one of its methodological principles into a new interface.

Poster Reading

The poster represents a synthesis of my interpretation rather than a summary of the project. Instead of reproducing the information contained in the website, it isolates the relational structure that I consider central to Calculating Empires: the production of knowledge through networks of historical relationships.

The diagram visualizes the convergence between genealogy, methodology, representation, and critical interpretation. By reducing the amount of text and emphasizing connections, the poster shifts attention from individual historical events toward the operations that organize them. It therefore functions as an analytical abstraction of the precedent rather than as a simplified version of the original map.

Website Reading

The website extends this interpretation through interaction. Conceived as a digital research archive, it abandons the conventional structure of a linear website in favor of a workspace composed of multiple independent records. Each window corresponds to a specific analytical operation—identification, genealogy, contextualization, methodological reconstruction, visual analysis, or critical interpretation—and can remain open simultaneously.

This interface reflects the way research is actually conducted: not through sequential reading, but through continuous comparison between heterogeneous documents. By allowing several analytical layers to coexist on the screen, the website encourages users to build their own investigative pathways. The interface itself becomes a methodological statement, translating the relational logic of Calculating Empires into a navigable research environment.

Final Reflection

Rather than treating Calculating Empires as a finished artifact, this precedent study understands it as a methodological model for investigating complex systems. My analysis suggests that the project's most significant contribution lies not only in the historical narrative it constructs, but in the operations through which that narrative becomes possible. Classification, visualization, navigation, and archival organization are not merely representational techniques; they are epistemological practices that shape how knowledge is produced. My website and relational diagram therefore reinterpret these operations, transforming the precedent itself into an interactive research apparatus through which new connections and critical readings can emerge.

02_Context

02_CONTEXT.TXTRESEARCH CONTEXT

Introduction

Calculating Empires was developed as a response to contemporary narratives that present artificial intelligence as an unprecedented technological revolution. Instead of treating AI as a recent innovation, the project reconstructs a much longer history of technological systems, tracing the political, economic, scientific, and cultural conditions that have shaped computational power over more than five centuries. By extending the temporal frame from the present back to 1500, the project challenges dominant accounts of technological progress and proposes genealogy as a method for understanding contemporary digital infrastructures.

Project Motivation

The project seeks to reveal that contemporary computational systems did not emerge in isolation. Artificial intelligence is presented as the latest manifestation of historical processes involving extraction, colonial expansion, labor, communication, military development, scientific classification, and the organization of knowledge. Rather than documenting isolated inventions, Calculating Empires investigates how multiple systems evolved together, producing the conditions that made modern computation possible.

Research Context

The project was developed through an interdisciplinary collaboration between researcher Kate Crawford and artist-researcher Vladan Joler. Their practice combines historical research, critical theory, archival investigation, and information visualization to examine the relationships between technology and power. Drawing from hundreds of historical sources across different disciplines, the project assembles a large-scale visual archive that encourages exploration rather than linear reading. This approach reflects a broader research culture that values open investigation, cross-disciplinary collaboration, and visual methods as tools for critical inquiry.

Exhibition Context

Calculating Empires exists simultaneously as a large-scale printed map, an interactive digital platform, and an exhibition installation. These multiple formats are fundamental to the project. The printed version encourages close reading of the complete genealogy, while the digital interface allows users to zoom, navigate, and explore individual relationships across the map. Rather than functioning as separate outputs, both formats support different modes of engaging with the same research.

Intended Audience

Although grounded in academic research, Calculating Empires addresses a broad audience that includes researchers, designers, architects, artists, students, curators, policymakers, and the general public. The project combines scholarly investigation with visual communication, making complex historical relationships accessible through an interactive interface and large-scale cartographic representation.

Context Summary

Produced during a period of renewed global attention toward artificial intelligence, Calculating Empires responds by shifting the discussion away from technological novelty toward historical continuity. Instead of asking what artificial intelligence will become, the project asks how centuries of political, economic, scientific, and technological developments have collectively produced the conditions in which contemporary AI exists today.

03_Visual System

03_VISUAL_SYSTEM.TXTVISUAL ANALYSIS

Introduction

The visual language of Calculating Empires is not a neutral method of displaying information but a critical instrument for constructing knowledge. Every graphical decision—from the organization of the grid to the hierarchy of labels, the density of information, and the interactive interface—supports the project's central argument: technology and power evolve through interconnected historical systems rather than isolated innovations. The visualization functions simultaneously as a map, an archive, and a research interface.

Ontology

What exists in the visualization?

The project is composed of multiple visual entities that together construct a navigable historical system.

These include:

  • Historical nodes
  • Temporal timeline
  • Thematic categories
  • Networks of relationships
  • Labels and annotations
  • Images and illustrations
  • Connecting lines
  • Interactive interface
  • Zoom and navigation tools

Rather than functioning independently, these elements create a layered representation where each component contributes to the reconstruction of historical relationships.

Composition

How is the information organized?

The visualization adopts a large-scale cartographic composition that prioritizes exploration over linear reading.

Information is organized through:

  • a continuous horizontal timeline spanning from 1500 to the present;
  • a vertical classification system grouping different domains of technological development;
  • multiple scales of reading, from planetary overview to individual historical events;
  • dense spatial organization encouraging comparative observation.

Instead of guiding users through a predefined sequence, the composition supports multiple reading paths.

Visual Language

Graphic vocabulary

The project employs a restrained visual language that emphasizes clarity while maintaining an extraordinary level of informational density.

Its primary graphic components include:

  • monochromatic palette
  • fine linework
  • hierarchical typography
  • minimal color coding
  • scientific diagram conventions
  • archival aesthetics
  • information graphics

The visual language avoids decorative elements and instead reinforces the project's documentary character.

Information Architecture

Rather than representing isolated objects, the visualization organizes knowledge through relationships.

Its architecture is based on:

  • classification
  • taxonomy
  • hierarchy
  • temporal sequencing
  • cross-referencing
  • thematic clustering

These organizational strategies transform the map into an interface for navigating historical complexity.

Interface

The digital version extends the printed map through interaction.

Users can:

  • zoom across multiple scales;
  • pan freely through the map;
  • access individual historical entries;
  • navigate thematic categories;
  • compare distant historical moments;
  • construct personalized reading paths.

Interaction therefore becomes part of the research process rather than a simple navigation tool.

Representation

The project deliberately avoids conventional historical timelines or chronological narratives.

Instead, it combines:

  • atlas
  • archive
  • network
  • taxonomy
  • systems diagram
  • historical cartography

The result is a hybrid representational system situated between scientific visualization and critical design.

Visual Argument

The visual system itself communicates the project's critical position.

The extreme density of information, the continuous timeline, and the interconnected networks suggest that technological development cannot be understood through isolated inventions or individual actors.

Instead, the visualization argues that technologies emerge through overlapping infrastructures, institutions, labor systems, and political structures distributed across long historical durations.

The map therefore functions not simply as a representation of history but as an argument about how history should be interpreted.

Key Visual Principles

  1. Density over simplification
  2. Relationships over objects
  3. Genealogy over chronology
  4. Exploration over linear reading
  5. Systems over individual technologies
  6. Archive over illustration
  7. Navigation over narration

04_Methodology

04_METHODOLOGY.TXTRESEARCH WORKFLOW

RESEARCH PROCESS / CE.04

METHODOLOGY

Introduction

Calculating Empires adopts an interdisciplinary research methodology that combines historical inquiry with information design. Rather than documenting technological history chronologically, the project develops a systematic process for identifying, organizing, and connecting heterogeneous historical materials into a coherent genealogical framework.

Research Strategy

The research begins by identifying historical developments that have contributed to the emergence of contemporary computational systems. Instead of focusing on individual inventions, the project investigates long-term relationships between technologies, institutions, political structures, economic systems, and forms of labor.

Data Organization

Collected materials are structured through a taxonomy that allows different historical processes to be compared across time. Categories function as analytical tools, enabling relationships to emerge between events that would normally be studied separately.

Visual Synthesis

Once organized, the information is translated into a unified visual framework. Rather than illustrating pre-existing conclusions, visualization becomes an active part of the analytical process, allowing complex relationships to be observed simultaneously across multiple temporal and thematic scales.

Interactive Exploration

The digital interface extends the research by allowing users to construct their own investigative paths. Navigation, zoom, and filtering support non-linear exploration, transforming the visualization into an open research environment rather than a fixed historical narrative.

Methodological Framework

The methodology can be summarized as a sequence of interconnected operations:

  1. Historical Research
  2. Source Selection
  3. Classification
  4. Relationship Mapping
  5. Visual Synthesis
  6. Interactive Exploration

05_Relational Diagram

Rather than representing a simple workflow or organizational hierarchy, this relational diagram reconstructs the network of relationships that produced Calculating Empires. It maps the convergence of authors, research practices, previous projects, methodological approaches, and conceptual influences that collectively shaped the project.

The diagram is organized into interconnected clusters corresponding to the main dimensions of the precedent study: Project Genealogy, Context, Visual System, Methodology, and Critical Reading. Each node represents a specific component of the research, while the connections describe how these components influence and reinforce one another.

Rather than following a linear sequence, the network reflects the interdisciplinary nature of Calculating Empires. Historical research, critical theory, information visualization, and investigative mapping operate simultaneously, producing a system in which knowledge emerges through relationships rather than isolated elements.

The relational diagram therefore functions as both an analytical synthesis of the precedent and a navigational framework for the website, allowing the different layers of the project to be explored as an interconnected research archive.
