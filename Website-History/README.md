# Layers of Analysis
## Calculating Empires: A Genealogy of Technology and Power Since 1500
**Kate Crawford & Vladan Joler (2023)**

---

# Ontological Analysis

## Overview

*Calculating Empires* is a large-scale visual research project that investigates the historical relationship between technological development and structures of political, economic, and social power. Rather than presenting technology as a sequence of independent inventions, the project reconstructs a genealogy in which communication systems, computational technologies, colonial expansion, scientific knowledge, bureaucratic institutions, and environmental extraction evolve together over more than five centuries.

The project challenges the common understanding of technology as a collection of neutral tools. Instead, it proposes that every technological artifact exists within a broader ecosystem of infrastructures, institutions, labor systems, legal frameworks, natural resources, and ideological structures. Technology is therefore understood as a relational system rather than an autonomous object.

---

### Historical Research

At its foundation lies an extensive interdisciplinary research process drawing from media history, colonial history, political theory, science and technology studies (STS), artificial intelligence research, economic history, and critical data studies. The visualization is therefore not merely a graphic representation but the final expression of a long process of historical investigation.

### Temporal Structure

The project spans a continuous timeline from approximately **1500 to the present**, illustrating how contemporary computational systems emerged through gradual historical transformations rather than sudden technological breakthroughs. Time becomes the primary organizational framework through which causal relationships and historical continuities become visible.

### Conceptual Framework

The visualization is organized around four fundamental concepts:

- Communication
- Computation
- Classification
- Control

These categories operate as interconnected systems rather than isolated themes. Communication technologies enable new forms of computation; computation supports systems of classification; classification reinforces mechanisms of political control; and these mechanisms, in turn, generate new technological infrastructures.

### Material Forms

The project exists across multiple formats:

- an interactive online platform,
- a large-scale printed installation,
- an academic publication,
- and an educational resource.

This multiplicity reflects its dual nature as both research and exhibition, allowing different modes of engagement with the same body of knowledge.

---

## Ontological Relationships

The ontology of *Calculating Empires* is based on relationships rather than objects. Individual technologies only acquire meaning through their connections with political institutions, military organizations, economic systems, scientific practices, and environmental resources.

For example, artificial intelligence is not represented as an isolated innovation. Instead, it appears as the latest stage of a much longer historical process involving census systems, statistical reasoning, colonial administration, industrial production, data collection, and algorithmic governance.

This relational ontology encourages viewers to understand technology as a complex assemblage in which social, political, ecological, and computational processes continuously interact.

---

# Historical and Contextual Analysis

## Historical Context

Produced in 2023, *Calculating Empires* emerged during a period marked by the rapid expansion of artificial intelligence, machine learning, platform economies, and large-scale data extraction. Public debate increasingly focused on issues such as algorithmic bias, digital surveillance, automation, misinformation, and the concentration of technological power within a small number of global corporations.

Rather than interpreting these developments as entirely new phenomena, Crawford and Joler argue that they should be understood within a much longer historical trajectory. By tracing the genealogy of computational systems back to the beginning of European colonial expansion around 1500, the project reveals how contemporary digital infrastructures inherit older systems of administration, classification, extraction, and control.

In this sense, the project responds not only to current debates about artificial intelligence but also to broader questions concerning the historical continuity of political power and technological development.

---

## Intellectual Context

The work is situated within a broad interdisciplinary framework that combines:

- Critical Data Studies
- Media Archaeology
- Science and Technology Studies (STS)
- Political Economy
- Postcolonial Studies
- Digital Humanities
- Information Design
- Critical AI Studies

Rather than adopting a deterministic view of technological progress, these disciplines examine how technologies participate in the production of knowledge, social organization, and political authority.

The project is particularly influenced by Michel Foucault's genealogical method, which investigates how systems of power emerge historically through complex networks of institutions, discourses, and practices rather than through singular events or inventions.

---

## Research Question

The central research question can be summarized as:

**How did contemporary computational systems emerge, and which historical structures continue to shape them today?**

Instead of constructing a linear history of technological innovation, the project demonstrates that communication systems, scientific knowledge, imperial expansion, industrialization, military infrastructures, and computational technologies have co-evolved over centuries.

Artificial intelligence is therefore presented not as a technological revolution but as one episode within a much larger historical genealogy.

---

## Intended Audience

The visualization addresses researchers, designers, architects, artists, historians, media scholars, students, and policy makers interested in understanding technology beyond purely technical perspectives.

Its extraordinary visual density intentionally slows down interpretation. Rather than providing simplified explanations, the project encourages viewers to explore relationships, discover unexpected historical connections, and actively construct their own reading of the map.

---

## Political Position

A central argument of *Calculating Empires* is that technology cannot be politically neutral.

Every technological system reflects historical choices concerning governance, labor, territorial expansion, resource extraction, social classification, and economic organization.

The project therefore critiques narratives of technological inevitability and instead presents computation as a historically contingent product of specific political and economic conditions.

---

# Project and Visual Aesthetic

## Overview

The visual language combines elements of information design, historical cartography, scientific illustration, archival graphics, systems diagrams, and contemporary data visualization. Rather than functioning as a conventional infographic, the project creates a visual research environment in which representation becomes a form of critical inquiry.

The graphic composition itself communicates the complexity of the subject matter by refusing simplification. Instead of isolating individual events, it continuously reveals relationships across different historical scales.

---

## Visual Organization

The visualization is organized around two principal spatial dimensions.

The **vertical axis** represents historical time, extending from approximately 1500 to the present day. This continuous chronology emphasizes historical continuity rather than discrete historical periods.

The **horizontal dimension** organizes thematic systems such as communication, governance, infrastructures, colonial expansion, scientific knowledge, military technologies, labor systems, environmental resources, and computation.

This dual organization allows multiple reading strategies. Viewers may follow technologies, institutions, concepts, historical periods, or political processes according to their own interests.

---

## Graphic Language

The project employs thousands of carefully illustrated graphic elements, including hand-drawn icons, connecting lines, annotations, symbols, diagrams, and typography.

Rather than prioritizing visual minimalism, the composition embraces density and complexity. The accumulation of visual information reflects the project's theoretical position that technological history cannot be reduced to simple narratives or isolated innovations.

---

## Reading Experience

Unlike digital interfaces optimized for speed and efficiency, *Calculating Empires* deliberately encourages slow observation.

The viewer constantly moves between macro and micro scales, discovering how seemingly distant historical events become interconnected through networks of communication, extraction, governance, and computation.

The visualization therefore functions less as an infographic and more as an intellectual landscape that supports exploration, comparison, and reflection.

---

## Visual References

The representational strategy draws inspiration from several traditions of visual knowledge production, including:

- Aby Warburg's *Mnemosyne Atlas*
- Charles Joseph Minard's statistical diagrams
- Charles and Ray Eames' *Mathematica*
- historical atlases
- scientific wall charts
- critical cartography
- encyclopedic visual systems

Like these precedents, *Calculating Empires* uses visual representation not only to communicate information but also to generate new forms of knowledge through spatial organization.

---

## Communication Strategy

The visualization intentionally avoids simplifying historical complexity. Instead, it presents technology as a continuously evolving network of relationships linking communication, computation, colonialism, ecology, labor, governance, and political power.

The project's aesthetic choices—its monumental scale, graphic density, layered organization, and non-linear reading paths—are inseparable from its intellectual argument. The visual form itself reinforces the central claim that technological systems can only be understood through the historical networks from which they emerge.

---

# Concluding Reflection

*Calculating Empires* demonstrates how information visualization can operate simultaneously as historical research, critical design, and theoretical argument. By transforming more than five centuries of technological, political, and economic history into a single interconnected visual landscape, the project expands the role of data visualization beyond representation. It becomes an analytical tool capable of revealing hidden genealogies, exposing structures of power, and encouraging viewers to critically reconsider the historical foundations of contemporary computational systems.
